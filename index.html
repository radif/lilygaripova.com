FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

What does a buyer's agent actually do for me?

A buyer's agent represents you at every stage: defining search criteria, scheduling tours, reviewing each property's MLS history and disclosures, identifying red flags before you write an offer, structuring the offer (price, contingencies, escalation), opening escrow, coordinating inspections and the appraisal, removing contingencies on a defensible timeline, and walking through closing documents. Lily Garipova's clients average 10–20 home tours over several weeks before writing an offer — the work happens between the showings, in the disclosure review and the city-permit verification, not just at the open houses.

Do I really have to sign a contract before viewing homes?

Yes — since the 2024 NAR settlement, California buyers must sign a written buyer-representation agreement before touring homes with an agent. The agreement specifies the agent's compensation source (typically the seller, not you) and the engagement scope. Lily Garipova offers flexible terms: single-property, time-limited, or full-search. She walks every clause with you in English or Russian before you sign — no surprise commitments.

Should I wait to buy in case prices drop?

The 2025–2026 Bay Area market is "split": some neighborhoods see multiple offers above asking, others require price reductions. Buyers have meaningful negotiating leverage in segments they didn't have for over a decade. Whether to wait depends on your finances, target neighborhood, and time horizon — not on a generic market call. Lily models your specific situation in a free consultation before recommending a position.

How do I know if the listing agent is hiding something?

The listing agent isn't lying when they pass along seller statements — that's their job. Verifying whether what the seller said is actually true is not their job, and would work against their client's interests. If you're buying, this work falls on your side or it doesn't get done. A recent example: Lily was on a $2.5M Campbell deal where the listing agent confirmed the ADU was "permitted." A 5-minute call to the City of Campbell revealed it wasn't. The next day a city inspector red-tagged the property — saving Lily's buyer $100K in renegotiation. One phone call.

What's CalHFA Dream for All and how do I get it?

Dream for All is a CalHFA shared-appreciation loan: the state contributes funds toward your down payment and closing costs in exchange for a share of future appreciation when you sell or refinance. Application windows are limited (the most recent was Feb 24 – Mar 16, 2026) and funds typically run out within weeks. Preparation is everything: get pre-approved with a CalHFA-listed lender, confirm income eligibility (under 120% AMI), and have your credit and documentation ready before the next window opens. Lily helps clients prepare 3–6 months ahead.

Why would I want a Russian-speaking agent if I speak English fine?

Real estate documents are dense — 100–300+ pages per transaction — and the technical vocabulary (contingencies, escrow, CC&R, contingency removal, EMD) doesn't translate cleanly. A Russian-speaking agent who is also bilingual at the document-review level can explain financial and legal implications in your native language while still negotiating fluently with the English-speaking other side. For Russian-speaking clients relocating from CIS countries, the difference between "translates at showings" and "fully bilingual at every step" is the difference between feeling lost and feeling protected.

The differentiator: Centermac's broker is also a licensed real estate attorney, providing continuous legal review of contract and contingency language built into every transaction, without an external attorney fee.
